WATER QUALITY MANAGEMENT FOR VANNAMEI SHRIMP (Penaeus vannamei)
Source: FAO Aquaculture, Wikipedia, Expert Guidelines

=== SAFE RANGES FOR KEY PARAMETERS ===

DISSOLVED OXYGEN (DO):
- Safe range: 4.0 - 8.0 mg/L
- Optimal: 5.0 - 7.0 mg/L
- Critical low: below 3.0 mg/L (shrimp will die within hours)
- Warning level: 3.0 - 4.0 mg/L (immediate action needed)
- DO is highest in early morning before sunrise (check at 5-6 AM)
- DO drops at night due to respiration, rises during day due to photosynthesis

TEMPERATURE:
- Safe range: 22°C - 32°C
- Optimal growth: 25°C - 30°C
- Critical: below 20°C or above 35°C
- Growth stops below 15°C
- White Spot Disease risk increases when temperature drops below 25°C or fluctuates rapidly

pH:
- Safe range: 7.5 - 8.5
- Optimal: 7.8 - 8.3
- Morning pH is lowest, afternoon pH is highest
- pH fluctuation >0.5 within 24 hours is stressful
- Low pH (<7.0) causes acid toxicity
- High pH (>9.0) increases ammonia toxicity

SALINITY:
- Vannamei can tolerate 0 - 40 ppt
- Optimal: 10 - 25 ppt
- Freshwater/low salinity farming possible at 2-10 ppt with adaptation
- Sudden salinity changes >5 ppt/day cause stress
- Indian inland farms typically use 5-15 ppt

ALKALINITY:
- Safe range: 80 - 150 mg/L as CaCO3
- Optimal: 100 - 150 mg/L
- Low alkalinity (<80 mg/L): pH unstable, crashes possible
- Add lime (CaCO3 or Ca(OH)2) to raise alkalinity
- Dose: 10-20 kg/acre to raise alkalinity by 10-15 mg/L

AMMONIA (Total Ammonia Nitrogen - TAN):
- Safe: below 0.5 mg/L
- Warning: 0.5 - 1.0 mg/L
- Toxic: above 1.0 mg/L
- Un-ionized ammonia (NH3) is the toxic form, higher at high pH and temperature
- Sources: feed excess, dead organic matter, shrimp excretion
- Control: reduce feeding, water exchange, probiotics

NITRITE (NO2):
- Safe: below 0.1 mg/L
- Warning: 0.1 - 0.5 mg/L
- Toxic: above 0.5 mg/L
- Interferes with oxygen transport in shrimp blood
- Control: water exchange, probiotics, salt addition (chloride competes with nitrite)

SECCHI DEPTH (Water Transparency):
- Optimal: 25 - 40 cm
- Too clear (>50 cm): insufficient phytoplankton, DO may drop at night
- Too turbid (<20 cm): excessive algae bloom, DO crash risk
- Ideal water color: green or light brown (indicating healthy phytoplankton)

=== DAILY MONITORING SCHEDULE ===

Early Morning (5:00 - 6:00 AM):
- Check DO (critical - lowest point of day)
- Check temperature
- Observe shrimp behavior at pond edges

Morning (7:00 - 8:00 AM):
- Check pH
- Observe water color and transparency (Secchi depth)
- Check feeding tray response

Afternoon (2:00 - 4:00 PM):
- Check DO (usually highest)
- Check pH (usually highest)
- Check temperature

Evening (6:00 PM):
- Feed tray check
- Observe shrimp surface activity

=== EMERGENCY ACTIONS ===

LOW DO (below 4.0 mg/L):
1. Turn on ALL aerators immediately
2. Stop feeding immediately
3. If available, add emergency aeration (paddlewheels, blowers)
4. Optionally add hydrogen peroxide (H2O2): 2-5 ml per cubic meter
5. Monitor every 30 minutes until DO stabilizes above 4.0 mg/L
6. Do NOT do water exchange during DO crisis (stress increases oxygen demand)

HIGH AMMONIA:
1. Reduce or stop feeding for 1-2 days
2. Add probiotics (Bacillus species)
3. Partial water exchange 20-30%
4. Add organic carbon source if biofloc system

pH CRASH:
1. Add agricultural lime (CaO) or dolomite
2. 10-20 kg/acre raises pH by 0.2-0.3 units
3. Apply in the morning when pH is naturally rising

pH HIGH (>9.0):
1. Partial water exchange
2. Reduce phytoplankton by reducing fertilizer
3. Use alum (aluminum sulfate) cautiously

=== WATER EXCHANGE GUIDELINES ===

Intensive farming:
- Early stage (DOC 1-30): 0-5% daily, minimal exchange
- Mid stage (DOC 31-60): 5-15% daily as needed
- Late stage (DOC 61+): 10-30% daily based on water quality

Never exchange >30% at once — causes stress
Always treat incoming water with chlorine (20-30 ppm) and neutralize before adding

=== SEASONAL CONSIDERATIONS (ANDHRA PRADESH/INDIA) ===

Summer (March-June):
- High temperature risk, ensure adequate aeration
- Water evaporation increases salinity
- DO stress more likely due to high temperature

Monsoon (July-September):
- Rain dilutes salinity rapidly — monitor daily
- Risk of pH crash due to acidic rainwater
- Disease outbreak risk higher
- Ensure pond bunds are strong

Winter (November-February):
- Temperature drops — growth slows
- White Spot Disease risk increases below 25°C
- Increase biosecurity during cold spells
